What You Are Actually Claiming

The cleanest version of the claim is this: IPL auction prices are not random, but they are not perfectly efficient either. A disciplined pre-auction model can explain a meaningful share of pricing, and the biggest deviations seem to cluster around scarcity, reputation, and marquee-player effects.

Your 30-second answer

I built a pre-auction valuation system for IPL players using only information available before the auction. It converts ball-by-ball IPL history and past auction records into batting, bowling, pedigree, scarcity, and market-context features, then predicts a fair benchmark price. I use that benchmark to identify where the market seems to pay too much or too little.

Your honesty sentence

This is not a claim that the model knows a player's one true price. It is a structured benchmark for comparing auction outcomes, and it is strongest as a decision-support tool, not an oracle.

End-To-End Pipeline

The notebook notebooks/ipl_auction_inefficiency_end_to_end.ipynb runs this in order: setup, file discovery, standardization, feature building, pre-auction merge, model training, mispricing analysis, post-season follow-up, and export.

1

Discover raw files

The project recursively scans data/raw/ and classifies files as auction, matches, or deliveries data. This happens in src/data_loader.py lines 14-29, 78-113.

2

Standardize messy schemas

Column names, player names, team names, seasons, and rupee values are normalized so different Kaggle files can be merged. See src/utils.py lines 13-223 and src/cleaning.py lines 24-136.

3

Resolve name mismatches

Auction names are matched to performance names using initials and fuzzy matching with a threshold of 0.88. That logic is in src/cleaning.py lines 153-238.

4

Build player-season features

Ball-by-ball data becomes batting, bowling, phase, career, recent-form, and pedigree features. See src/features.py lines 47-205.

5

Create the pre-auction dataset

Auction year Y is matched to performance season Y - 1, which is the most important anti-leakage choice in the project. See src/features.py lines 208-246.

6

Train and compare models

The project tests a scorecard baseline, linear regression, random forest, gradient boosting, and a two-stage market-premium model. See src/modeling.py lines 341-595.

7

Convert predictions into mispricing

The final output calculates modeled prices, article prices, and undervaluation or overvaluation labels. See src/modeling.py lines 609-839.

8

Generate charts and follow-up review

The narrative layer includes charts and a post-2025 realized review that is useful for storytelling but separate from model training. See src/visuals.py lines 110-278 and src/features.py lines 316-453.

ML Concepts You Need To Understand

You do not need to be a technical specialist. You do need to be fluent in the basic vocabulary below.

Supervised learning

A method where you show the algorithm historical examples with known answers. Here the known answer is historical auction price.

Target variable

The thing you are predicting. In this project the target is log(1 + price_in_inr), not raw price.

Features

The input signals used to predict price: runs, wickets, strike rate, role, scarcity, prior auction price, leadership proxies, and more.

Log transform

Big auction prices are extremely skewed. Logging compresses the scale so one superstar does not dominate the loss function.

Train/test split

You train on older years and test on the latest years. This is closer to the real forecasting problem than random splitting.

Imputation

Many players have missing values. The pipeline fills numeric gaps with medians and categorical gaps with the most frequent value.

One-hot encoding

Text categories like role and team must be converted into machine-readable columns before model training.

Overfitting

A model that memorizes history rather than learning general patterns. The future-year holdout is meant to reduce this risk.

Proxy variable

A stand-in for an unobserved concept. Example: hard-coded leadership and captaincy lists are proxies for intangible market value.

MAE

Mean absolute error. Lower is better. It tells you the average size of the prediction error in log-price space.

RMSE

Root mean squared error. It penalizes big misses more heavily than MAE.

R^2

The share of variance explained by the model. Here it is about 0.50 on holdout years, which is respectable for noisy auction behavior.

Anti-leakage line you should memorize

merged["performance_season"] = merged["auction_year"] - 1

This line in src/features.py line 214 proves the model uses the season before the auction, not the season after it.

out["target_log_price"] = np.log1p(out["price_in_inr"])

How to explain gradient boosting in plain English

It is an ensemble model that builds many small decision trees in sequence. Each new tree tries to correct the mistakes of the earlier ones. That makes it good at learning nonlinear relationships, like the fact that the value of wicketkeeping or death bowling is not constant across all players and auction years.

Why not just use linear regression?

Auction pricing is full of interactions: a player's value depends on role, nationality, scarcity, prior reputation, and current market conditions all at once. Linear regression struggles when those relationships bend and interact.

The article's "fair price" is not the same thing as the selected ML model's raw predicted price. The code first creates an intrinsic scorecard floor, then a raw selected-model prediction, then a business-facing article fair price that blends model output, role anchors, auction history, and premium multipliers for scarcity and marquee effects.

Features The Model Uses

Batting output

Runs, balls faced, strike rate, batting average, boundary-ball percentage, dot-ball percentage, runs per innings, and phase strike rates.

Bowling output

Wickets, economy, bowling strike rate, dot-ball percentage, powerplay wickets, death wickets, and phase economies.

Career depth

Prior seasons, career runs, career wickets, three-year sums, and career-best seasons before the auction.

Role and scarcity

Wicketkeeper, all-rounder, death bowler, control bowler, left-arm bowler, elite Indian pacer, and role-year medians.

Market context

Inflation index, spend index, mega-auction proxy, previous auction price, auction-history mean, and career peak auction price.

Soft-value proxies

Leadership, captaincy, title-winning captain flags, Indian batter premium, and SENA-overseas premium.

Business translation: skill + scarcity + reputation + context Committee watchout: many of these are hand-engineered Weakness: no injury, age, domestic form, or international T20 data

Assumptions Built Into The Project

Explicit assumptions

  • Pre-auction performance from season Y - 1 is an appropriate information set for auction year Y.
  • Historical IPL ball-by-ball data is a meaningful proxy for player value.
  • Scarcity and reputation can be approximated with hand-crafted proxy variables.
  • Future years are the right holdout test for real forecasting.

Implicit assumptions

  • Name harmonization is accurate enough that the matched subset is reliable.
  • The 41.8% retained sample is representative enough to support directional conclusions.
  • Past auction price carries information rather than just market hype.
  • Role inference overrides are more helpful than harmful.
Best self-aware sentence: the project is strongest as a structured benchmark on the matched subset, and weaker as a claim about the entire IPL auction market.

Strengths, Weaknesses, And Risks

Strength

Real forecasting logic

It uses historical information only and tests on future auction years rather than random row splits.

Strength

Rich feature engineering

It captures recent form, career depth, role scarcity, market context, and prior auction reputation.

Strength

Business framing

The output is not just predictive accuracy. It translates into mispricing, franchise discipline, and player archetype premiums.

Weakness

Low coverage

Only 577 of 1,381 raw auction rows are retained after matching. That is the biggest empirical limitation.

Weakness

Heavy hand-engineering

Many proxies are subjective: leadership lists, marquee flags, elite pacer lists, and article premium caps.

Weakness

Limited explainability

The repo does not include SHAP values or formal feature-importance interpretation for the final model.

How to frame the strengths

Say that the project combines sports analytics with pricing logic. It is not just a cricket stat exercise; it is an attempt to model how real organizations price scarce talent in a constrained market.

How to frame the risks

Say openly that the model is partly empirical and partly judgmental. That honesty makes the project more credible, not less.

Questions You Are Very Likely To Get

1. What is the business question here, beyond cricket fandom?

The business question is whether a talent market allocates capital efficiently. IPL auctions are a useful setting because every player has a visible clearing price, teams face constraints, and player value depends on both hard performance and soft scarcity. That makes it a clean applied pricing problem.

2. Why is this an MBA-type project rather than just a data science project?

Because the core issue is strategic resource allocation under uncertainty. The ML is just the tool. The real topic is market inefficiency, premium pricing, reputational effects, and disciplined decision-making.

3. Did you use any information that teams would not have known before the auction?

Not in the training dataset. The key anti-leakage rule is performance_season = auction_year - 1 in src/features.py line 214. The post-2025 realized review is only a later follow-up, not part of model training.

4. Why did you predict log price instead of price?

Auction prices are extremely skewed. A few huge deals can dominate learning if you model raw rupees directly. Log price makes the training problem more stable and makes errors more proportional.

5. Why should anyone trust a model with R^2 around 0.50?

Because auction price is a noisy market outcome affected by hidden information, injuries, fit, budgets, and bidding wars. Explaining about half the variance using only pre-auction structured data is meaningful. The right benchmark is not perfection; it is whether the model is useful as a disciplined decision aid.

6. Why is coverage only 41.8%, and does that weaken the project?

Yes, it weakens the breadth of the claim. Coverage is limited by conservative name matching and incomplete histories. The upside is that the matched rows are more reliable. The honest conclusion is that results apply most strongly to the retained subset, not necessarily to the full auction universe.

7. Why are there hard-coded player lists for captaincy, elite pacers, and wicketkeepers?

Because some market drivers are real but not cleanly available in the raw datasets. The project approximates them with proxies. That improves realism but introduces subjectivity. I would describe this as a hybrid model: data-driven core plus domain priors.

8. Is the published article using the same thing as the best ML model?

Not exactly. The published article uses a calibrated fair-price layer in src/modeling.py lines 624-839. That layer blends model output, history anchors, role anchors, and premium multipliers. If asked bluntly whether it is "pure ML," the correct answer is no.

9. So are you moving the goalposts after seeing the results?

The fair criticism is that the article price is partly judgmental. The defense is that the article is trying to estimate fair market value, not only intrinsic cricket output. The right way to present it is as analyst-calibrated valuation, not as a single raw algorithmic truth.

10. What is the difference between "overvalued" and "bad player"?

Overvalued means expensive relative to modeled fair value, not untalented. A player can still be excellent and yet have been bought at an aggressive price. That distinction is important for Shreyas Iyer, Arshdeep Singh, and Rishabh Pant.

11. Why did gradient boosting beat the two-stage market model?

Probably because the direct single-stage model captured enough nonlinear structure already, while the two-stage design added extra complexity without enough signal. It is a good example of why elegant theory does not always beat simpler empirical performance.

12. What would you improve in version two?

Better entity resolution, age and injury data, domestic and international T20 stats, yearly cross-validation beyond a single holdout design, uncertainty intervals, and explainability tools such as SHAP.

13. What is the biggest risk of bias in this project?

Selection bias from the matched subset, plus subjectivity from hand-coded market-premium proxies. Both could systematically tilt conclusions toward better-known or better-documented players.

14. What is the single strongest thing in the project?

The temporal discipline. It uses prior information to explain future auction prices. That makes the setup much more defensible than a retrospective "story after the fact."

15. What is the single weakest thing in the project?

The gap between the raw model and the article's calibrated fair price, combined with low row coverage. That is the area a skeptical committee member should push on, and you should be ready to answer it directly.
